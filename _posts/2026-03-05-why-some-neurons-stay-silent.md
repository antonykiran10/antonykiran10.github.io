Not every neuron fires. Some sit at the edge of threshold, poised but never triggered — and that silence might be just as meaningful as the signal itself.

## The idea of silence

We tend to think of the nervous system as a system of signals — action potentials racing down axons, neurotransmitters crossing synapses, circuits humming with activity. But silence is not the absence of information. In many cases, it *is* the information.

This is something I think about a lot while watching zebrafish larvae under the microscope. The circuits are there, the connections are formed — but not all of them fire when you expect them to.

## What we're still learning

The mechanisms that keep a neuron quiet are just as elaborate as those that make it fire. Inhibitory interneurons, intrinsic membrane properties, the precise balance of excitatory and inhibitory inputs — all of these conspire to hold some cells at rest.

Understanding *why* a circuit is assembled the way it is requires understanding both what fires and what doesn't.
